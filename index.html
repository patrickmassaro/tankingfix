About This Tool & Methodology
Why occupancy % instead of raw attendance?
Raw attendance figures are almost meaningless for cross-team comparison because arenas vary enormously in size — the Palace of Auburn Hills held 22,076, Madison Square Garden holds 19,812, and the Kia Center holds 18,846. A team drawing 17,000 per game means something completely different depending on whether their building seats 18,000 or 22,000. Occupancy % (attendance ÷ arena capacity) normalizes for this, making a Detroit crowd and an Orlando crowd directly comparable. It also automatically adjusts when teams move arenas mid-dataset.
Why a 10-year rolling baseline instead of 3 or 5?
A shorter window gets gamed too easily. If a team tanks for three years, their 3-year baseline degrades alongside their attendance — by year four, the tank has become the new normal and the trigger stops firing. A 10-year window is long enough that a sustained rebuild can't erode its own floor. It also captures a full competitive cycle for most franchises, smoothing out single outlier seasons (championship runs, injury years) that would distort a shorter average. The tradeoff is that truly new franchises or relocated teams need time to build a baseline — which is why the trigger requires at least two prior valid seasons before it can fire.
Why start in 2004–05 and not earlier?
Three reasons. First, reliable digital attendance records with consistent capacity tracking become much cleaner post-2004 — earlier data requires significant manual reconciliation across arena changes, franchise relocations, and inconsistent reporting standards. Second, the current revenue sharing framework didn't exist in its modern form until the 2005 CBA, so modeling penalties against a system that didn't exist yet would be misleading. Third, 2004–05 captures the full arc of the modern tanking era: the pre-lottery-reform period, the post-Hinkie Process era, and the post-pandemic rebuild wave. Twenty seasons is enough to draw meaningful conclusions without introducing pre-modern data noise.
Why are COVID and lockout seasons excluded?
The 2011–12 lockout (66 games) and 2020–21 COVID season (no fans) are excluded from trigger calculations because attendance in those years reflects external constraints rather than owner behavior — penalizing a team for empty seats during a pandemic or a labor stoppage would be obviously wrong. The 2019–20 season (partial, COVID-interrupted) is also excluded. These seasons still appear in the chart as gray bars for context. One acknowledged limitation: the COVID gap likely masks what would have been additional consecutive trigger years for OKC and other rebuilding teams, which would have escalated their penalties significantly.
How are revenue sharing estimates calculated?
The RS receipt estimates shown in the table are approximations derived from Forbes annual team revenue data, modeled against the RS formula (teams contribute ~50% of local revenue to the pool and receive back an allocation based on league average payroll). The 2021–22 figures are calibrated against the verified Woj/ESPN memo showing a $404M combined pool and $10.46M per-team luxury tax distribution. Earlier and later years are estimated from Forbes revenue trajectories. These carry roughly ±$5M uncertainty — sufficient for illustrating the policy's scale, but not audited financials. Teams shown as "—" in the RS column are estimated net payers into the system and would not receive a penalty under this proposal.
Why announced attendance and not scanned gate data?
This tool uses official announced attendance figures — the same numbers ESPN and the league report publicly — because that's what's available historically. Announced attendance is the number of tickets distributed, not bodies in seats, and is known to be systematically higher than actual turnstile counts. A 2018 WSJ investigation found scanned attendance averaging 71% of announced for college football. The NBA has scan-based data for every game but doesn't release it publicly. An actual implementation of this policy would use verified scanned gate counts — which would likely make the occupancy figures lower across the board, potentially tightening the triggers. Consider the numbers here a conservative floor.
